# Literature Review

## Theoretical Review

The study is grounded in Albert Bandura's **Social Learning Theory (1977)**, which holds that people learn behaviors more effectively by observing and practicing modeled scenarios than by relying solely on the consequences of their own real-world mistakes. This framework informed the decision to let users safely "practice" responding to fraud scenarios inside the app.

- **Globally:** Simulation-based training is increasingly used by financial institutions in the UK and Europe to teach customers how to spot sophisticated fraud.
- **Regionally:** East African platforms like Kenya's *iWhistle* made progress, though they often require high digital literacy.
- **Locally:** Mobile money fraud remains a well-documented problem in Kampala, where interventions like *Tonfera* and *Beera Steady* focus on media-based expert advice.

CyberScam Lab bridges these contexts by applying Social Learning Theory to a localized, voice-guided mobile platform.

## State of Practice

- **Globally:** Countries such as Nigeria and Kenya use mobile apps and SMS alerts to help users detect and report scams.
- **Regionally (East Africa):** Tools like M-Pesa fraud alerts provide real-time warnings, but typically require reliable internet access and strong digital skills.
- **Locally in Kampala:** State-of-practice relies heavily on passive awareness — UCC's *Tonfera* campaign and telecom-operator SMS warnings raise awareness but offer no hands-on training.

CyberScam Lab improves on this by offering an offline-first, voice-guided simulation tailored to fraud tactics prevalent in the Kampala ecosystem.

## State-of-the-Art / Similar Existing Systems

Three existing systems were benchmarked to identify functional gaps:

**System A — Interactive Storytelling by Innovations for Poverty Action (Uganda).** Voice-based storytelling using Interactive Voice Response (IVR) technology and radio programs. Presents real-life scenarios but lacks a dedicated mobile app, offers low user interaction, and has no progress-tracking mechanism.

**System B — MobileMoneyFraudAwareness.com.** A web-based platform with articles, alerts, and local-language material. Supports some offline access and basic progress tracking through saved content, but lacks interactive, scenario-based decision-making.

**System C — Uganda's Current Awareness Setup (SMS and Media Campaigns).** The combined efforts of *Tonfera* and *Beera Steady* — mass-media alerts, radio broadcasts, and expert discussions. Broad in reach but limited by a passive learning approach and no personalized or interactive training.

## Conclusion

The literature review shows that while several systems address mobile money fraud, most are limited by a lack of interactivity, accessibility, or local relevance. Global and regional tools often require internet connectivity or high digital literacy that many Ugandan users lack. Locally, awareness efforts rely on passive media campaigns with no hands-on practice. CyberScam Lab addresses these gaps by combining voice-guided simulations with an offline-first approach and localized content — a practical education tool tailored specifically for mobile money users in Kampala.
